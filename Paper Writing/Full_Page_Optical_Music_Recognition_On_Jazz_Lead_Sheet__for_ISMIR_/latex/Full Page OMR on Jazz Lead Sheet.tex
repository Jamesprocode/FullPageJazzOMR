\begin{abstract}
% just a working abstrct
Optical music recognition (OMR) of handwritten jazz lead sheets remains a challenging problem due to the variability of chord notation and the limited availability of annotated data. While recent work has shown promising results on pre-segmented staff-level lead sheets transcription, full-page handwritten lead sheet recognition has remained unexplored. We present the first full-page OMR system for handwritten jazz lead sheets, adapting the Sheet Music Transformer with a curriculum learning strategy that progressively trains the model on inputs of increasing length, from single staff up to complete pages. To evaluate the system, we construct a detect-and-concatenate pipeline as a full-page baseline and propose an evaluation methodology that can separate harmonic transcription performance from melodic transcription performance. Experiments show that our full-page system consistently outperforms a comparison system working a pre-segmented staves. %with the largest gains in chord recognition and line level error rate, confirming that page-level context is beneficial for chord symbol transcription of jazz lead sheets.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Optical Music Recognition (OMR) systems have advanced rapidly in recent years, enabling reliable transcription of printed staff notation into symbolic formats that support useful downstream tasks such as automatic transposition, part extraction, score editing, and computational music analysis. These tools provide meaningful convenience for performers and educators by digitizing traditional sheet music workflows. Handwritten jazz lead sheets, however, present a distinct challenge for OMR. Unlike fully notated classical scores, lead sheets combine staff notation with chord symbols written above the staff, requiring transcription of both melodic and harmonic information, while handwriting introduces substantial visual variability beyond the printed notation settings. 
When no editable digital version is available, even simple tasks such as generating transposed parts ---essential for musicians playing transposing instruments such as saxophone or trumpet--- become unnecessarily cumbersome. %The automatic recognition of jazz lead sheets would, for example, simplify jazz rehearsals and jam-sessions for musicians playing transposing instruments such as saxophone or trumpet, where a lead sheet requires transposition while reading the score.  
Thus, reliable OMR for jazz lead sheets would provide an immediate and practical benefit by converting the image into symbolic notation that can be edited, transposed, and redistributed.

Recently, Martinez-Sevilla et al. \cite{martinez-sevilla_optical_2025} presented one of the first approaches to handwritten jazz lead sheet OMR by introducing handwritten jazz lead sheet dataset and adapting a transformer-based system to recognize pre-segmented staff-level images. This study reports that chord-symbol recognition remains a challenge in the staff-level formulation of handwritten jazz lead sheet OMR, as most transcription errors in their qualitative analysis are related to chord symbols rather than melody. The main reason for this is that chords are handwritten in shorthand that varies across writers (e.g., \texttt{C$\triangle$7}, \texttt{Cmaj7}, and \texttt{CM7} denote the same chord). These difficulties raise the question of whether chord-symbol transcription may benefit from moving beyond isolated staff-level inputs. As recent literature notes, segmenting a page into individual staves inherently destroys visual context, making it impossible for models to assess cross-staff interactions \cite{RiosVila2026FullPageOMR}. Furthermore, segmentation-based approaches suffer from error propagation, where segmentation mistakes cascade into the transcription stage. By bypassing this two-stage pipeline, full-page input provides richer global context than staff-level crops, preserving complex reading orders across multiple systems and capturing cross-staff interactions that are otherwise lost with staff-level input. This full-page approach might be especially beneficial in jazz lead sheets for capturing harmonic patterns and chord progressions that extend across line breaks, as well as correctly aligning chord symbols that are vertically misaligned with their corresponding notes. 

In this study, we investigate full-page OMR for handwritten jazz lead sheets and its effect on chord-symbol transcription. Our main contributions are:
\begin{compactitem}
\item The presentation of the first \textbf{end-to-end full-page OMR system} for handwritten jazz lead sheets transcribing both melodic and harmonic content. We show that this approach ---trained with a curriculum learning strategy--- reliably outperforms a state-of-the-art staff-level transcription approach.
\item The introduction of an \textbf{extended set of chord-oriented evaluation metrics} for OMR to enable a finer understanding of model capabilities and error types.
\end{compactitem}

\section{Related Work}

% start with really broad OMR and early object detection method
The field of Optical Music Recognition (OMR) studies computational methods for transcribing images of musical scores into machine-readable symbolic formats such as Humdrum, MusicXML, and MEI \cite{calvo-zaragoza_understanding_2021}. Early OMR was typically formulated as a multi-stage document analysis pipeline, following the steps of staff-line detection and removal, symbol segmentation, symbol classification, and musical reconstruction \cite{blostein1992critical, bainbridge1997extensible, bainbridge2001challenge}. Within this framework, music symbol recognition was approached with detecting vertical lines, note head, and beams using models like k-nearest neighbors, hidden Markov models and support vector machines \cite{rebelo2010optical, baro2016compound}. This pipeline-based view was influential in early OMR and obtained reasonable results. However, it faced robustness challenges for higher complexity tasks such as polyphonic input, handwritten scores, and other visually diverse sheet-music inputs \cite{hartelt2022medieval}. 

% going to deep learning models with YOLO, SSD and RetinaNet, and CNN stuff as object retrival
With the rise of deep learning, OMR adopted object-detection frameworks from computer vision, adapting general detectors such as YOLO \cite{redmon2016yolo}, SSD \cite{liu2016ssd}, RetinaNet \cite{lin2017focal}, and Faster R-CNN \cite{ren2016faster}. Noteworthy is the  work of Pacha et al.\ establishing a deep-learning baseline for general music object detection using Faster R-CNN, showing that deep learning detectors could identify a broad set of symbol classes directly from score images \cite{pacha2018baseline}. Subsequent studies extended these ideas to more specialized handwritten settings: de Vega et al.\ applied Mask R-CNN to handwritten four-part harmony exercises\cite{devega2022omr}, while Paul et al.\ used an ensemble of transfer-learned CNNs, including ResNet, GoogleNet, and DenseNet, for isolated handwritten music symbol classification \cite{paul2022ensemble}. Together, these works marked a transition from symbol classification to CNN-based deep learning approach, while still treating OMR as an object-detection task.

% going to sequence to sequence transformer models
A different deep-learning direction reformulates OMR not as object detection, but as direct transcription from score images to symbolic sequences. In this line of work, Calvo-Zaragoza et al.\ explored sequence-based OMR for printed monophonic music using Convolutional Recurrent Neural Networks (CRNNs), in which a CNN extracts visual features from the score image and an RNN predicts the corresponding sequence in a custom serialized sequence \cite{calvo-zaragoza2018end}. The models were trained with Connectionist Temporal Classification (CTC) loss, allowing staff images to be transcribed directly into symbol sequences without explicit information about the location of the symbols in the image \cite{alfaro-contreras2023homophonic}. This same CRNN--CTC formulation was later extended to handwritten music that improved symbol-level accuracy on handwritten notation benchmarks \cite{calvo-zaragoza_handwritten_2019}. However, to address the limitations of CTC when dealing with simultaneous musical symbols, Baró et al.\ established a new baseline for handwritten Western music targeting handwritten scores using independent frame-wise dense layers and a Smooth L1-loss function to predict pitch and rhythm simultaneously \cite{baro_optical_2019}. For more complex polyphonic piano notation, Mayer et al.\ argue that reliable end-to-end OMR also depends on the consistency of the symbolic target, introducing Linearized MusicXML (LMX) as a stable textual representation for training and evaluation \cite{mayer_practical_2024}. Similarly breaking the monophonic barrier, Rios-Vila et al.\ introduced the Sheet Music Transformer (SMT), a CNN-to-Transformer architecture that directly predicts Humdrum \texttt{**kern} encoding from score images, successfully handling complex polyphonic structures at the staff level \cite{rios-vila_sheet_2024}. The input data used by studies of the sequence-based OMR systems mentioned above are pre-segmented single-staff samples, which reduce layout complexity and shorten the prediction sequence. A notable exception is recent work on full-page recognition. Rios-Vila et al.\ extended the work of SMT to full-page recognition with curriculum training, positioning it as the first end-to-end OMR approach designed to handle complex polyphonic structures at the page level \cite{RiosVila2026FullPageOMR}.


% moving to jazz lead sheets
Beyond input granularity, OMR difficulty mostly depends on the notation target being transcribed. Jazz lead sheets recognition present a closely related yet distinct challenge and is relatively underexplored. Unlike classical polyphonic scores in common Western notation, lead sheets combine a largely monophonic melodic staff with chord symbols, introducing an additional recognition target. Martínez-Sevilla et al.\ presented the first handwritten jazz lead sheets dataset and baseline system for this task by adapting the SMT architecture to transcribe pre-segmented staff images \cite{martinez-sevilla_optical_2025}. 

Building on these lines of work, our study focuses on the intersection of three challenges that have largely been treated separately in prior OMR research: handwritten notation, full-page transcription, and jazz lead sheet recognition. 


\section{Methodology}
This section presents our methodology for full-page OMR of handwritten jazz lead sheets. Our study aims to answer two questions. 
First, can an end-to-end model directly transcribing a complete handwritten lead sheet outperform a model relying on pre-segmented staff images? 
Second, in the absence of prior work on full-page handwritten jazz lead sheet recognition, what is an appropriate methodology and metrics to capture different aspects of recognition performance compare to a baseline?
To answer these questions, we first describe the dataset and data-cleaning steps, then introduce the detect-and-concatenate baseline and the proposed full-page models, both of which build from the same staff-level SMT checkpoint. Finally, we define the standard and chord-oriented metrics used to evaluate the systems.

\subsection{Dataset}
We use the JazzMus dataset~\cite{martinez-sevilla_optical_2025}, a collection of 163 unique jazz standards consisting of synthetic and handwritten scores aligned with Humdrum and MusicXML encodings.\footnote{In this study, all output is represented in Humdrum notation; see \cite{huron1999music} for the Humdrum reference guide.} The synthetic portion includes 326 full-page renderings generated using MuseScore with two font styles (Classical and MuseJazz). The handwritten portion includes 293 pages produced by jazz professionals and students who manually copied the synthetic lead sheets by hand. All multiple copies of the same piece are restricted to the training set, preventing leakage of identical musical content across splits. The dataset also provides bounding boxes for each individual staff for each staff on the full page. 

\subsubsection{Data cleaning}
\label{sec:data cleaning}
The ground truth annotations in JazzMus were obtained through conversion from MusicXML to Humdrum \texttt{**kern}, and the resulting Humdrum encodings contain a number of conversion-related artifacts. Therefore, we perform two data cleaning steps to improve consistency for training. 
First, some chord symbols in the \texttt{**mxhm} spine that were incorrectly represented as comments rather than chord annotations and, thus, not parsed as ground truth. We corrected these annotations in 14 full-page samples to ensure that the chord symbols are properly encoded in the chord spine for training and evaluation.
Second, the original staff-level bounding boxes for the synthetic subset reused crop coordinates from the handwritten subset, which led to inaccurate cropping of individual staves and inconsistent inclusion of chord symbols above the staff. We therefore corrected the synthetic bounding boxes so that each staff crop is properly aligned and consistently captures the chord annotations above the staff. 
We contributed these fixes back to the dataset repository through pull requests.\footnote{\url{https://huggingface.co/datasets/PRAIG/JAZZMUS} (accessed February 23, 2026).}

It should be noted that the handwritten part of the data reflects the natural variation introduced by the manual copying of the lead sheets. Minor discrepancies, such as differences in accidentals and slur markings, occur between the image and the symbolic annotation. These variations are retained in the dataset, as they provide a realistic setting for evaluating model robustness to handwritten input.

\subsection{Models}
We adopt the Sheet Music Transformer (SMT) \cite{rios-vila_sheet_2024} as the base model architecture for all experiments. SMT is an encoder–decoder architecture for image-to-sequence music transcription, using a ConvNeXt encoder to extract visual features and a Transformer decoder to auto-regressively generate sequences in Humdrum notation. Unlike previous approaches relying on CTC loss, the SMT decoder predicts the probability of each symbol conditioned on both the visual features and the previously generated tokens with teacher forcing applied during training. Following the staff-level study \cite{martinez-sevilla_optical_2025}, we adopt the medium word-level tokenization, which was identified as the best-performing tokenization scheme.


\subsubsection{Baseline: Staff-Level Recognition with YOLO Segmentation}
Because prior work on handwritten jazz lead sheet OMR is studied at the staff level rather than the page level, there is currently no established baseline for evaluating full-page handwritten jazz lead sheet.
As our study introduces full-page handwritten jazz lead sheet transcription, we construct a detect-and-concatenate baseline to serve as a comparison system for our proposed model. As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:baseline_flowchart}, the pipeline first detects individual staves on the page, then transcribes each staff independently using the staff-level SMT recognizer, and finally concatenates all the staff predictions into a page-level Humdrum output. This baseline preserves the staff-level recognition setup of earlier work while extending it to full-page input. It therefore provides a natural comparison system for assessing whether page-level training improves performance beyond what can be achieved by staff-level recognition alone.

\subsubsection{Staff Segmentation}
Page images are first preprocessed with resizing and de-skewing to normalize scale and correct page rotation.
To perform the staff segmentation, we adopt YOLOv11s \cite{dvorak2024staff}, an object detection model fine-tuned specifically for the music staff-detection task. However, the raw bounding-box detections cannot be passed directly to the staff-level recognizer and  require additional post-processing. We identified three problems: missed staff detections, duplicate detections of the same staff, and bounding boxes that are too narrow vertically to capture chord symbols above the staff. To obtain staff crops that better match the pre-segmented single-staff inputs expected by the staff-level recognizer, we apply a post-processing procedure to refine the raw detections:
First, missed staff detections are recovered by analyzing the spacing between consecutive boxes: the median Euclidean distance between adjacent top-left corners is used as a reference, and any pair of consecutive boxes whose top-left distance exceeds 1.8$\times$ the median triggers the insertion of one virtual bounding boxes. Each virtual box's top-left corner is placed at an evenly spaced position along the straight line connecting the top-left corners of the two surrounding detections, and its width and height are set to the median box dimensions in that page.
Second, YOLO occasionally predicts two overlapping boxes (one partial-width box and one full-width box) for the same staff. We identify these cases using one-dimensional vertical Intersection over Union (IoU) \cite{yu2016unitbox}, a standard object-detection measure of overlap computed as the shared area divided by the total area covered by both boxes. Two boxes are flagged as duplicates when the vertical IoU exceeds 0.6. In this case, we remove the partial-width box.
Finally, the raw bounding-box detections are adjusted by identifying inter-staff gap and assigning 25\% of the gap to the upper crop and 75\% to the lower crop. This expands each lower staff crop upward so that chord symbols written above its staff lines are included, while keeping adjacent crops non-overlapping.

\subsubsection{Staff-Level Recognition}
After the segmentation process, each cropped staff image is fed to the SMT model pretrained on the staff level study \cite{martinez-sevilla_optical_2025}. The staff predictions are concatenated into a full-page output: the first staff's header and spine declarations are preserved, subsequent staves have their Humdrum headers stripped, and all staves are joined with line-break notation in Humdrum (\texttt{!!linebreak:original}) separators, with spine terminators appended at the end. We then evaluate the concatenated results against the full-page ground truth.

\subsubsection{Proposed Model}
To support full-page transcription, we extend the output vocabulary with a dedicated \texttt{<linebreak>} token so that the model can explicitly represent staff boundaries and generate multi-staff outputs. This increases the vocabulary size from 154 to 155. Accordingly, the input and output of the decoder embedding layer are increased by one. In both layers, the staff-level pretrained weights, used in the baseline parameters, are preserved, while the parameters associated with the new \texttt{<linebreak>} token are initialized randomly. Beyond this change to the input embedding and output layer, the model architecture remains unchanged. The main methodological challenge is therefore not redesigning the model architecture itself, but bridging the gap between staff-level pretraining and full-page recognition. To address this, we investigate two curriculum learning strategies that progressively expose the model to longer and more structurally complex inputs. 

\subsubsection{Masking Curriculum}
The masking curriculum achieves the transition to full-page transcription through vertical masking. which reveals larger top portions of each page while masking out the lower content. We apply a 9-stage curriculum learning strategy. At each stage $k \in \{1, \dots, 9\}$, the training data are modified to reveal exactly $k$ staves. This allows the model to gradually adapt to larger image and annotation inputs and longer output sequences as the stages progress. 

The masking curriculum data is constructed from JazzMus full-page scans. For each full-page image, we generate a set of top-$N$ crops by retaining only the top $N$ staves and cropping away the remainder. For example, if a lead sheet page contains 8 staves, we generate 8 masked versions of that page, each retaining the top $N$ systems for $N$ = 1, \dots , 8. For each image crop, the ground truth annotation is truncated to the first $N$ staves, deleting all subsequent symbols, and ending with the final Humdrum spine terminator *-. Although JazzMus full-page scans contain up to 11 staves, we restrict the masking curriculum to 9 stages. This choice results from the staff-count distribution of the full-page subset: the majority of pages contain between 6 and 8 staves (207 of 294 pages), whereas only 28 pages contain 9 or more. Treating 9-, 10-, and 11-staff pages as separate stages would therefore add little useful variation, since these pages already function as near-complete or complete full-page inputs while contributing only a small number of additional training examples. Thus, we group all pages with 9 or more staves into stage 9, which serves as the final full-page stage. This data preparation procedure yields 1,688 training samples and 105 validation samples. At each curriculum stage, the training subset contains 245 handwritten and 278 synthetic samples, while the validation subset contains 16 samples. The test set is used only at the final stage and consists of 32 unmodified full-page samples. 

Training begins from the pretrained staff-level SMT checkpoint and proceeds through the curriculum schedule using a staged advancement strategy. At stage $k$, the training and validation set include only samples with $k$ staves. Once a sample reaches its maximum available number of staves, it remains unchanged as a full-page sample in all subsequent stages. The stage advances once the validation metrics (see ~\ref{sec:metrics}) falls below 20\% for 3 consecutive validation epochs without improvements. The 20\% threshold serves as a readiness check, since preliminary runs showed that stages typically converged around 15\%. At the final stage ($k$ = 9), all samples are full-page without any masking and training continues until early stopping with a patience of 10 validation checks. 

A direct consequence of this training strategy is that when the stage shifts from $k-1$ to $k$, samples from earlier stages disappear from the training sample entirely, and the model risks forgetting shorter-input behavior it previously learned. To monitor this, after each stage we perform a full validation sweep over all nine validation subsets. Preliminary runs without replay showed that models trained on later stages degraded substantially on shorter-input subsets, indicating forgetting of earlier-stage behavior. To mitigate this, we introduce experience replay: at each stage, a fraction $r$ of samples from earlier stages is retained in the training pool, where $r$ denotes the replay ratio. We study four replay settings, with replay ratios $r \in {0, 0.25, 0.50, 1.00}$, to examine how retaining earlier-stage samples affects stability and final performance.


\subsubsection{Stacking Curriculum}
The stacking curriculum follows the same 9-stage curriculum training procedure as the masking approach, differing only in the way the training data are prepared. Rather than masking full-page scans, we construct longer input samples by synthesizing multi-staff examples from individual staff crops. At stage $k = 1$, the data consist of the training set of 1,688 handwritten and 1,872 synthetic staff-level entries, and a validation set of 105 handwritten samples, which is all individual staves derived from the training and validation set. For stages $k = 2, \dots, 9$, the same number of training and validation stacked samples are constructed by selecting one staff crop and sampling $k-1$ additional staves from other examples. The selected staves are resized to a uniform height with aspect ratio preserved, padded to a common width, and vertically concatenated into a single multi-staff image resembling a full-page lead sheet. %For example, at stage $k = 4$, each stacked sample contains four staves: one reference staff and three additional staves. 
The corresponding ground-truth sequence is formed by concatenating the per-staff annotations in stacking order. The annotation of the first staff is kept with its original Humdrum header and spine declarations, while subsequent staff annotations have their headers removed and are joined using the \texttt{<linebreak>} token with the final spine terminators. At the final stage ($k$ = 9), 245 handwritten and 278 synthetic real full-page images are additionally appended to the training pool so that the model is exposed to authentic page-level layout before evaluation. This results in a total of 32,563 training samples and 945 validation samples across the curriculum. The test set identical to the one for the masking curriculum: 32 unmodified full-page samples. Training begins from the same pretrained staff-level SMT checkpoint and proceeds through the stacking curriculum using the same staged advancement and final stage early-stopping strategy as in the masking setting. To account for increased training time due to the larger number of training samples, experience replay is applied with a fixed ratio of $r = 0.25$, balancing training time and not forgetting earlier stage training.

Because the stacking curriculum relies on synthetically generated training and validation data, the number of samples produced at each stage is itself a design choice. Here, we fix this number to match the total number of available staff-level examples, so as to remain consistent with the pretrained staff-level checkpoint. An interesting direction for future work could be to study how different sample counts at each stage influence training dynamics and final performance. A practical drawback of increasing training set size is that the larger number of generated samples in the stacking curriculum significantly increases the computational cost of training, both in memory usage and in training time.

\subsubsection{Experimental Setup}

For the masking curriculum, the model is optimized with AdamW using a learning rate of $5 \times 10^{-5}$ and 150 warmup steps. Validation is performed every 10 epochs. A batch size of 8 is used for stages 1--8, while stage 9 uses a batch size of 1 and a reduced learning rate of $5 \times 10^{-6}$ due to the increased memory requirements of full-page inputs. For the stacking curriculum, the same optimizer is used. Stages 1--5 are trained with a batch size of 8 and a learning rate of $5 \times 10^{-5}$. Stages 6--8 use a batch size of 4 with the same learning rate, and stage 9 and 10 uses a batch size of 1 with a reduced learning rate of $5 \times 10^{-6}$.

All experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA H200 GPU using BF16 mixed precision.



\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}
Following prior work in OMR and end-to-end music transcription~\cite{calvo-zaragoza_understanding_2021, rios-vila_sheet_2024, martinez-sevilla_optical_2025, RiosVila2026FullPageOMR}, we report three edit-distance-based error rates as overall measures of model performance: Character Error Rate (CER), Symbol Error Rate (SER), and Line Error Rate (LER), computed as the Levenshtein edit distance between the prediction and ground truth, divided by the total number of ground-truth units at the corresponding level and multiplied by 100. CER is computed at the character level and indicates individual character errors. SER is computed at the symbol level, assessing the correctness of individual musical symbols, although it may still treat partially correct predictions as a whole edit-distance substitution error. LER is computed at the line level: a predicted line is counted as a substitution if it differs from the ground truth by even a single character. In our setting, LER implicitly evaluates line-level structural errors, such as assigning a chord symbol to the wrong note line, but it does not distinguish between minor and major mismatches within a line. To better isolate melodic accuracy, these metrics are also computed on the melodic spine only. These melody-only metrics are denoted with the subscript Melo: 
$\mathrm{CER}_{\mathrm{Melo}}$, $\mathrm{SER}_{\mathrm{Melo}}$, and $\mathrm{LER}_{\mathrm{Melo}}$.

While the traditional OMR metrics provide an overview of transcription accuracy, they are insufficient for isolating harmonic performance as melodic and harmonic errors are treated equally. To allow for differentiation between melodic and harmonic recognition performance, we introduce two chord-oriented metrics based on extracting chord symbols from the **mxhm spine into an ordered list and disregarding the spacing between them. 
The two proposed metrics are defined as:

\begin{compactitem}
\item $SER_\mathrm{Root}$: the Levenshtein distance over the number of chord symbols in the ground truth between predicted and ground truth chord sequences which are reduced to their root-note tokens only. This metric measures errors in chord-root recognition independently of the remaining chord specification.

\item $SER_\mathrm{Chord}$: the Levenshtein distance over the number of chord symbols in the ground truth between full predicted and ground truth chord sequences. Under this metric, a chord is counted as correct only when its complete symbolic form is transcribed correctly, including root, quality, extensions, and inversion when present.
\end{compactitem}

These two metrics are intended to capture different levels of harmonic correctness. In jazz lead sheets, the chord root conveys harmonic progression and leads the song structure, so root-note errors are often more consequential than mistakes in extensions or other symbolic details. $SER_\mathrm{ROOT}$ therefore evaluates whether the model recovers the harmonic backbone of the piece, while $SER_\mathrm{Chord}$ provides a stricter measure that also requires correct transcription of chord quality, extensions, and inversion. Comparing the two helps distinguish between errors that change the underlying harmony and errors that miss finer chordal detail.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
We organize the results around two subsections. First, we report overall full-page recognition performance using the standard OMR edit-distance metrics: character error rate (CER), symbol error rate (SER), and line error rate (LER). This establishes whether the proposed full-page curriculum models improve over the detect-and-concatenate staff-level baseline. Second, because jazz lead sheets contain both melodic notation and chord symbols, we separate the evaluation by musical function: we compute standard metrics on melody spine and the proposed chord-oriented metrics. This allows us to determine whether the gains come primarily from melodic transcription, harmonic transcription, or both.

The overall full-page recognition results are shown in Table~\ref{tab:overall}. This result matches the previous staff-level study with minor increase within $\pm 2-3\%$. Among the masking curriculum models, performance improves consistently as the replay ratio increases. Without replay ($r=0$), the model performs worse than the baseline in CER and SER, although its LER is slightly lower than the baseline. A small amount of replay ($r=0.25$) produces only minor improvement, while $r=0.50$ brings CER and SER much closer to the baseline and further reduces LER. The best-performing model is the masking curriculum with full replay ($r=1.00$), which slightly improves over the baseline in CER and SER,\note{did you do a pairwise significance test?}, while reducing LER substantially. The consistent LER improvement suggests that full-page training helps the model produce better line-level structure. Since LER is the metric most sensitive to structural mismatches, and the only standard metric that indirectly reflects chord–melody alignment, this improvement may indicate better alignment of chord symbols. In contrast, CER and SER improve only when sufficient replay is used. One possible explanation is that higher replay ratios substantially increase the amount and diversity of training data by retaining more prior-stage samples, which supports stronger character- and symbol-level performance in later curriculum stages.

The stacking curriculum, on the over hand, performs worse than both the baseline and the masking curriculum on the overall metrics. Although synthetic stacking exposes the model to longer inputs and more training data, this result suggests that the generated examples appear to differ too substantially from real full-page layouts. Because the stacked staves are sampled from unrelated examples, this procedure also disrupts global musical context, such as key continuity and chord progressions across the staff, making generalization to real full-page test samples more difficult. We experimented fine-tuning the final stacking method checkpoint on only the complete full page training sample, but the result improved only marginally.
The limited improvement from fine-tuning suggests that simply exposing the model to real full-page samples after stacking curriculum training is not sufficient to overcome the mismatch introduced during earlier stacking training. One possible explanation is that the number of real full-page examples is relatively small compared to the large amount of synthetically stacked data, making it difficult for the model to fully adapt to authentic page layouts. This indicates that the distribution gap introduced by stacking persists even after fine-tuning.

\section{Discussion}
To better understand why full-page training improves chord recognition, we analyze model behavior through three questions: what types of chord errors are reduced, where on the page the improvements occur, and when full-page context provides the greatest advantage.
\subsection{Chord-Confusion Patterns}
The first analysis focuses on the root-letter confusions most frequently produced by the baseline and the best full-page model. This analysis is not intended to exhaustively categorize all chord-recognition errors; instead, it highlights the most frequent and substantial error patterns that distinguish the two systems. Table~\ref{tab:chord_confusions} lists the five most common root-letter confusion pairs in the baseline together with their counts under the full-page model. All five drop substantially: the largest absolute reductions appear on B$\leftrightarrow$D ($-25$) and C$\leftrightarrow$G ($-24$), both of which are visually ambiguous in handwriting due to similar stem and bowl shapes. The relative reductions are also substantial, with B$\leftrightarrow$D falling by $93\%$ and the remaining four pairs reduced by  $21\%$ to $63\%$. We also notice chord prediction mistakes on accidental. While the Baseline model confuses chords with accidentals like \# and $\flat$ in the chord symbols (B$\rightarrow$B$\flat:$ 14 occurrences, C$\rightarrow$C\#: 12 occurrences, A$\rightarrow$A$\flat:$ 10 occurrences), the best-performing masking model eliminates the first two error types entirely, while A$\rightarrow$A$\flat$ is reduced to 3 occurrences.

\subsection{Effect of Line-Break Proximity}
\note{if we run out of space mark this following paragraph as candidate for deletion or dramatic shortening.}
We further tested whether the improvement from full-page training is concentrated near line breaks, where staff-level recognition may lose harmonic continuity across adjacent staves. To examine this, we aligned the predicted chord streams from the baseline and full-page model to the ground-truth chord stream using edit distance. For each ground-truth chord, we recorded whether it was correctly predicted by each model and grouped errors according to the chord’s token distance from the nearest <linebreak> token. The relative reduction in chord errors from the baseline to the full-page model remains broadly consistent across distances: 47.1\% at distance 1, 46.5\% at distance 2, 55.6\% at distance 3, 53.2\% at distance 4, and 39.7\% at distance $5{+}$, with an overall reduction of 48.1\%. Because chords closest to a line break ($d{=}1{-}2$) do not show larger improvements than chords farther from a line break, the harmonic gains cannot be attributed primarily to improved handling of line-break regions. Instead, the results suggest a broader page-level effect, in which full-page context improves chord recognition across the page rather than only near system boundaries.

\subsection{Effect of Page Size}
How the two systems behave on the number of staves per test page $N$ provides another way to test the role of full-page context. Figure~\ref{fig:per_n_results} plots $\mathrm{SER}_{\mathrm{Melo}}$ and $\mathrm{SER}_{\mathrm{Root}}$ against $N$ for both models. On denser pages ($N\geq 7$), which account for the majority of the test set, the full-page model consistently outperforms the baseline on chord recognition, with the gap widening as $N$ grows; melodic error rates stay close for both systems. On pages with fewer staves, the detect-and-concatenate baseline remains competitive, occasionally outperform full-page system on melodic transcription. This suggests that the benefit of full-page training scales with page complexity: as pages contain more staves and more harmonic context, the full-page model's access to global context translates into a larger advantage.

\section{Conclusion}
We have presented the first full-page OMR system for handwritten jazz lead sheets, comparing a detect-and-concatenate baseline with two curriculum learning approaches that progressively train the model on inputs of increasing length. The masking curriculum with full experience replay ($r=1.00$) outperforms the baseline on all evaluation metrics, with the most pronounced improvements in LER and Chord SER. Along with the detailed analysis in the discussion section, this suggest that page-level training is beneficial for chord symbol transcription with a stronger improvements on lead sheet with higher number staves. The replay experiments show that retaining more prior-stage data during curriculum training leads to stronger full-page recognition performance, suggesting that the amount and diversity of training examples play an important role in improving prediction accuracy. The proposed chord-specific metrics, Root SER and Chord SER, also provide a useful insight to evaluate chord-symbol transcription, revealing harmonic recognition improvements that are not fully captured by aggregate edit-distance metrics.

As noted in ~\ref{sec:data cleaning}, the annotation errors introduced in the data collection process cannot be fully overlooked. Correcting transcription errors from annotation inconsistencies is an important direction for future work. More broadly, data scarcity remains the primary bottleneck: the Jazzmus dataset contains only 293 handwritten pages, limiting both training diversity and evaluation reliability. A natural next step is to construct a larger corpus by re-engraving standards from the Real Book with Humdrum annotations and collecting corresponding handwritten copies, which would provide both greater scale and cleaner ground truth.
Existing kern-encoded jazz corpora such as the Charlie Parker Omnibook transcriptions used in~\cite{baker_idiomaticism_2016} could serve as a starting point for building such a resource.

Finally, vision-language models (VLMs) pretrained on large-scale image-text data offer a promising alternative to training OMR systems from scratch. Exploring transfer learning from VLMs to music score recognition could reduce dependence on OMR-specific pretraining data and allow later fine-tuning to adapt more flexibly to the annotation style of the target dataset, rather than being constrained by the annotation conventions used to train the pretrained OMR model. This direction may be especially useful for full-page sequence-to-sequence handwritten OMR more generally, where models must handle long visual inputs, extended symbolic outputs, and substantial variation in handwriting style and page layout.
